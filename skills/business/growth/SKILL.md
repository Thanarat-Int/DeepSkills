# Growth

Distribution is half the product. A great thing nobody hears about loses to an average thing everyone sees. Growth is not noise on every channel — it's finding the one or two that fit *this* product, compounding them with measured experiments, and saying it in words that make people act.

## Operating stance

- **Channel–product fit before scale.** Most channels won't work for most products. Find the one or two where your users already are and the economics work, then go deep, not wide.
- **Retention is the real engine.** Acquiring users who churn is a bucket with a hole; pouring in more water is the expensive way to stay empty. Fix the leak before scaling the inflow.
- **Benefit before feature, one job per piece.** Copy leads with the outcome the reader gets, not the mechanism, and drives a single action. Specific beats clever; superlatives are noise.
- **Everything is an experiment with a kill switch.** Treat channels and campaigns as hypotheses with budgets — cut losers, compound winners.

## Workflow

### 1. Nail positioning
- One sentence: who it's for, the problem it solves, why it's different. If this isn't sharp, no channel or copy will save it. Identify where these users already spend attention.

### 2. Choose 1–2 channels deliberately
- Match channel to product (high-intent search for known-problem tools; communities/content for niches; referral for social products; partnerships for B2B; app stores for mobile). Sanity-check unit economics — a channel that can't pay back is a hobby. Pick the fewest you can execute well.

### 3. Design the funnel
- Map awareness → interest → signup → activation → retention → referral; find the weakest conversion step (measure via **product**). Fixing the worst rate usually beats adding a channel.

### 4. Write the words
- For each asset, pin the brief: reader, stage, the *one* action. Lead with the single most compelling benefit (becomes the headline/subject). Structure: landing = headline(benefit) → subhead → proof → objection → one CTA; email = subject → first line → one idea → one CTA; post = hook → value → soft CTA.
- Write tight: active voice, "you", cut a third, handle the top objection, CTA is an action with the benefit attached. Read it aloud. Every claim true and specific.

### 5. Launch & run as experiments
- Plan the launch moment (where, assets, sequence) — but a launch is a spike, not a strategy; plan what sustains it. For each channel/campaign: hypothesis, boxed budget/time, success metric, decision rule. Cut losers fast; reinvest in winners.

### 6. Record what works
- Log channels tried, cost, result, winning copy/playbook (`docs/growth/…`). Growth compounds only when written down.

## Hand-off
- Need to measure funnel/channel/copy results, A/B a headline → **product** (analytics).
- Users arrive but don't pay / pricing unclear → **monetize**.
- Acquired users churn → **product** (discovery: why).
- Reframe the same content for execs/stakeholders → **management-talk**.

## Operating rules
- **Focus on one or two channels.** Spreading thin across every platform is how a small team wins at nothing.
- **Fix retention before scaling acquisition.** Growth on a leaky funnel just burns money faster.
- **Know your unit economics.** If a user costs more to acquire than they're worth, scaling the channel scales the loss.
- **Benefit first, one CTA, cut until it bleeds.** If the headline describes the feature not the reader's win, rewrite it; padding loses readers.
- **Sound human; never lie to sell.** Second person, active voice, no jargon; hype that overpromises wins the click and loses the customer.
- **Position sharply or nothing converts.** If you can't say who it's for and why it's different in one line, fix that first.
